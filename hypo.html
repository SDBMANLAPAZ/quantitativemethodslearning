üîç Sources of Research Problems

Where do research problems come from?

Research problems emerge from various sources in our academic and professional environments. Understanding these sources helps researchers identify meaningful questions worth investigating.

Major Sources:

  • Literature Gaps: Unresolved questions or contradictions in existing research
  • Professional Practice: Real-world challenges and problems in your field
  • Theory Development: Testing or extending existing theories
  • Technology & Innovation: New tools creating new research possibilities
  • Social Issues: Current societal problems needing investigation
  • Personal Curiosity: Questions arising from observations and experiences

üìã Basic Criteria for Research Problem Formulation

What makes a good research problem?

Not every question makes a good research problem. Strong research problems meet specific criteria that ensure they're worth investigating and feasible to study.

Essential Criteria:

  • Significance: The problem should matter to the field or society
  • Feasibility: Possible to investigate with available resources and time
  • Clarity: Clearly defined and specific, not vague or overly broad
  • Originality: Adds something new to existing knowledge
  • Ethical: Can be studied without causing harm
  • Researchable: Can be answered through systematic investigation

üî¨ Types of Hypotheses

Understanding Hypotheses

A hypothesis is a testable prediction about the relationship between variables. Different types serve different research purposes.

Main Types:

Directional vs. Non-directional

Directional: Predicts the direction of relationship

Example: "Students who study more will score higher"

Non-directional: Predicts a relationship exists but not the direction

Example: "There is a relationship between study time and test scores"

Null vs. Alternative

Null (H‚ÇÄ): No relationship exists

Example: "Study time has no effect on test scores"

Alternative (H‚ÇÅ): A relationship exists

Example: "Study time affects test scores"

Other Important Types:

  • Simple: One independent and one dependent variable
  • Complex: Multiple independent and/or dependent variables
  • Associative: Variables are related but no causation is claimed
  • Causal: One variable causes changes in another

‚ö° Formulating Hypotheses from Research Problems

From Problem to Hypothesis

Converting a research problem into testable hypotheses is a crucial skill. It requires identifying variables and predicting their relationships based on theory and logic.

Step-by-Step Process:

  1. Identify variables in your research problem
  2. Determine which is independent (cause) and dependent (effect)
  3. Review theory/literature to understand likely relationships
  4. Write the null hypothesis (no relationship)
  5. Write the alternative hypothesis (predicted relationship)
  6. Make it testable and specific

Example Transformation:

Research Problem: "How does social media usage affect sleep quality in college students?"

Variables: IV = Social media usage (hours/day), DV = Sleep quality (scale score)

H‚ÇÄ: "Social media usage has no effect on sleep quality in college students."

H‚ÇÅ: "Increased social media usage is associated with decreased sleep quality in college students."

‚≠ê Criteria for a Good Hypothesis

Quality Checklist

A well-formulated hypothesis meets specific criteria that make it scientifically valuable and testable.

Essential Criteria:

  • Testable: Can be verified through observation or experimentation
  • Specific: Clearly defines variables and expected relationships
  • Based on Theory: Grounded in existing knowledge or logical reasoning
  • Simple: Not overly complex or containing multiple unrelated predictions
  • Falsifiable: Can potentially be proven wrong
  • Relevant: Addresses the research problem meaningfully
  • Measurable: Variables can be quantified or categorized

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • ‚ùå Using vague terms: "Students will perform better"
  • ‚ùå Making value judgments: "Method X is the best approach"
  • ‚ùå Being too broad: "Technology affects learning"
  • ‚ùå Including multiple predictions: "X affects Y and Z affects A"
