Quantitative Research as Scientific Inquiry

Exploring systematic approaches to knowledge building

What is Quantitative Research?

A systematic investigation of phenomena by gathering quantifiable data and performing statistical, mathematical, or computational techniques to analyze it. It focuses on measuring variables, testing hypotheses, and identifying patterns or relationships in a structured and objective way.

Different Approaches to Research Inquiry

1. Quantitative Research

Purpose: To test hypotheses, quantify variables, and generalize results from a sample to a population.

Characteristics:
  • Uses numerical data
  • Emphasizes objectivity, measurement, and statistical analysis
  • Often employs structured tools (e.g., surveys, experiments)
Methods:
  • Surveys
  • Experiments
  • Longitudinal or cross-sectional studies
Example:

Measuring the effect of a new teaching method on student test scores using pre- and post-tests.

2. Qualitative Research

Purpose: To explore meanings, experiences, and social phenomena in depth.

Characteristics:
  • Uses non-numerical data (words, images, observations)
  • Focuses on context, interpretation, and understanding
  • Flexible and iterative design
Methods:
  • Interviews
  • Focus groups
  • Ethnography
  • Case studies
  • Narrative analysis
Example:

Interviewing cancer survivors to understand their emotional journey during treatment.

3. Mixed Methods Research

Purpose: To combine strengths of both quantitative and qualitative approaches for a more comprehensive understanding.

Characteristics:
  • Integrates numerical and textual data
  • Can be sequential (one phase follows another) or concurrent (both collected simultaneously)
Designs:
  • Explanatory: quant → qual
  • Exploratory: qual → quant
  • Convergent: parallel integration
Example:

First conducting surveys to measure stress levels among teachers, then interviewing a subset to explore causes in depth.

4. Action Research

Purpose: To solve practical problems and improve practices through collaborative inquiry.

Characteristics:
  • Cyclical process: plan → act → observe → reflect
  • Involves stakeholders (e.g., teachers, community members)
  • Emphasizes change and empowerment
Common in:

Education, organizational development, community projects

Example:

A teacher researching and refining classroom management strategies over a semester.

Four Classifications of Quantitative Research

Descriptive Research

Purpose: To describe and interpret the current status of individuals, groups, or situations.

Key Feature: Answers 'what is happening?' without manipulating variables.

Example: A survey describing students' study habits.

Correlational Research

Purpose: To investigate the relationship between two or more variables.

Key Feature: Determines if variables are related but does not establish causation.

Example: Studying the relationship between hours of sleep and academic performance.

Experimental Research

Purpose: To identify cause-and-effect relationships by manipulating the independent variable.

Key Feature: Uses random assignment to control and experimental groups.

Example: Testing a new teaching method on randomly assigned student groups.

Causal-Comparative Research

Purpose: To identify possible cause-and-effect relationships after the fact by comparing groups.

Key Feature: No manipulation of variables; studies existing differences.

Example: Comparing academic performance of male and female students.

Research Problems & Hypotheses

Interactive Learning Experience

Sources of Research Problems

Research problems emerge from various sources in our academic and professional environments. Understanding these sources helps researchers identify meaningful questions worth investigating.

Major Sources:

  • Literature Gaps: Unresolved questions or contradictions in existing research
  • Professional Practice: Real-world challenges and problems in your field
  • Theory Development: Testing or extending existing theories
  • Technology & Innovation: New tools creating new research possibilities
  • Social Issues: Current societal problems needing investigation
  • Personal Curiosity: Questions arising from observations and experiences

Basic Criteria for Research Problem Formulation

Strong research problems meet specific criteria that ensure they're worth investigating and feasible to study.

Essential Criteria:

  • Significance: The problem should matter to the field or society
  • Feasibility: Possible to investigate with available resources and time
  • Clarity: Clearly defined and specific, not vague or overly broad
  • Originality: Adds something new to existing knowledge
  • Ethical: Can be studied without causing harm
  • Researchable: Can be answered through systematic investigation

Types of Hypotheses

A hypothesis is a testable prediction about the relationship between variables. Different types serve different research purposes.

Directional vs. Non-directional

Directional: Predicts the direction of relationship

Example: "Students who study more will score higher"

Non-directional: Predicts a relationship exists but not the direction

Example: "There is a relationship between study time and test scores"

Null vs. Alternative

Null (H₀): No relationship exists

Example: "Study time has no effect on test scores"

Alternative (H₁): A relationship exists

Example: "Study time affects test scores"

Defining and Measuring Concepts and Variables

Think About This:

"How would you measure happiness?"
"Can intelligence be measured the same way as height?"

Key Definitions

Concept

Abstract idea or mental construct (e.g., intelligence, stress, leadership)

Variable

Measurable representation of a concept that can vary or take on different values

Operational Definition

The process of converting a concept into measurable indicators

Example: From Concept to Variable

  • Concept: Academic performance
  • Operational Definition: GPA on a 4.0 scale
  • Variable: GPA scores (0.0–4.0)

Classification of Variables

A. By Role in Research

Independent Variable (IV)

The cause or predictor variable that is manipulated or controlled by the researcher.

Dependent Variable (DV)

The effect or outcome variable that is observed and measured for changes.

Confounding Variable

Extra factor that may influence the IV-DV relationship and needs to be controlled.

Example:

Study time (IV) → Test score (DV), but prior knowledge (confounding variable)

B. By Nature of Values

Quantitative Variables
Discrete Variables

Countable values

Examples: No. of children, No. of errors

Continuous Variables

Measurable values

Examples: Height, weight, temperature

Qualitative Variables (Categorical)
Nominal Data

Categories with no inherent order

Properties:
  • No ordering possible
  • Cannot be compared on a scale
  • No arithmetic operations
Examples:

Gender, Blood type, Marital status, Colors, Days of the week

Ordinal Data

Categories with rank order but unequal intervals

Properties:
  • Follows sequential ordering
  • Can be compared on a scale
  • Some arithmetic operations possible
Examples:

Grades (A,B,C,D), Reviews (excellent, good, fair, poor), Educational qualifications

Scales of Measurement

Understanding scales of measurement is crucial for choosing appropriate statistical analyses and interpreting results correctly.

Scale Properties Examples Math Operations
Nominal Identity only Gender, Blood type Mode
Ordinal Identity + Order Education level, Likert scales Mode, Median
Interval Identity + Order + Equal Intervals Temperature (°C), IQ scores Mean, SD
Ratio Identity + Order + Equal Intervals + Absolute Zero Height, Weight, Income All stats + Ratios

Types of Measuring Instruments

Research employs various instruments to collect data, each suited for different types of variables and research contexts.

1. Direct Observation

Measuring variables through direct observation of behaviors or physical characteristics.

Examples: Height measurement, behavior frequency counts

2. Self-Report Instruments

Collecting data through participants' own responses and perceptions.

Examples: Surveys, interviews, rating scales

3. Performance-Based Tests

Assessing abilities, skills, or knowledge through standardized tasks.

Examples: IQ tests, achievement tests, skill assessments

4. Physiological Measures

Recording biological or physiological indicators of variables.

Examples: EEG, blood pressure, heart rate

5. Existing Records

Using pre-existing documentation and archival data.

Examples: Academic transcripts, medical records, historical data

Validity and Reliability

Two crucial concepts in evaluating the quality of measuring instruments.

VALIDITY

Does the instrument measure what it claims to measure?

Types of Validity:
  • Content Validity: Covers all aspects of the concept
  • Construct Validity: Measures the theoretical construct
  • Criterion Validity: Predicts or correlates with other measures
  • Face Validity: Appears to measure what it should

RELIABILITY

Does the instrument produce consistent results?

Types of Reliability:
  • Test-Retest: Consistency over time
  • Internal Consistency: Items measure same construct
  • Inter-rater: Agreement between raters
  • Split-Half: Correlation between test halves
